About the Vision

IconSyntax is about three things:

  • an online library of searchable, audible, translatable icons,
  • an online sentence generator for interactive narratives,
  • an appeal to artists to help us build the icon library.

ILS is under construction, using these three assets: Iconopedia, a vocabulary of over twenty thousand icons in every word category, Instant English, a patented program which provides interactive lessons in icon, and a website—iconsyntax.org—to connect to artists, developers and linguists and ask for their help.

Executive Summary

Instant English and Iconopedia have been in development and use for three decades. Its inventor, MB Guinan, has recently added NFT’s and a new website named IconSyntax.

It is our intent to invite Artists from everywhere in the world to add to our database. Since words evoke different images to different speakers, artists from cultures outside the U.S. can supply synonyms that are ethnically specific.

What Is Syntax?

To acquire a new language, babies, and second-language learners need to first collect words. But a word, by itself has no intentionality. It needs to combine. The first combinations that make sense are phrasal verbs, like the ones at the bottom of each of these pages. A visual syntax requires an image for each word or phrase, and punctuation. This means we need millions of images.

We will have an icon generator and a committee of editors to critique new icons. The work can be done by a multitude of artists, using social media, and we will make a global effort to find them.

What Makes a Good Icon?

Good icons mean what they picture and are instantly understood without words—“toilet”, “food”, “campsite”, “skiing”, etc.

Icons need to be small enough to line up in a sentence. But font-sized icons can be too small because there aren’t enough pixels for detail. Abstract icons need to be learned, such as the hamburger icon for menu.

Just like font families, icons sets should have the same style, the same size, and a single light/dark color scheme.

An icon can have as many synonym images as one can imagine.

What Are Icon Parts of Speech?

  • NOUNS and modifiers are pictograms. Gray-scale photos can be used for proper nouns, such as people or cities, or landmarks, or art.
  • VERBS: See examples of phrasal verbs on each page here. A verb against a grayed background clues the user that the verb is past tense.

What Are Icon Parts of Speech?

(continued)

  • FUNCTION WORDS—like conjunctions or prepositions—are links between words that give the sentence context.
  • PUNCTUATION AND NUMBERS are included in the icon library.
  • COLOR is only used when it is a semantic necessity, such as flags or color names.

It worked great, so I invented and patented a program that read icon sentences to users while they typed from dictation. The City Colleges of Chicago funded the Windows 95 version and used it for over a decade. I drew about 20,000 GIFs that I used in stories, songs, tests and forms at Truman College.
